Background

Why Full-Body Musculoskeletal Control Is Hard

Human movement emerges from neural control, muscle activation, muscle-tendon force transmission, skeletal motion, postural stability, and interaction with the environment. This process is high-dimensional, strongly nonlinear, and governed by coupled dynamic, contact, and physiological constraints.

Existing optimization-based methods provide physical consistency and interpretability, but are often computationally expensive and sensitive to initialization. Learning-based controllers can support fast deployment and adaptation, but may struggle with constraint satisfaction and biomechanical plausibility. Differentiable dynamics offers a bridge between these two paradigms.

Research Aim

Build an Optimization-Supervised Control Pipeline

The project aims to connect muscle activation, muscle-tendon force transmission, joint torque generation, whole-body motion, and external contact in a unified computational framework.

Learning-Based Whole-Body Control

Use optimization as a physics supervisor to guide policies with residuals, constraints, and feasible reference trajectories.

Methodology

Three Connected Modules

Differentiable Musculoskeletal Modelling

The musculoskeletal model is represented as a tree-structured multibody system composed of rigid bone segments, joints, contact points, and muscle attachment sites. It supports forward kinematics, inverse dynamics, forward dynamics, contact modelling, and Jacobian-based force mapping.

  • Recursive rigid-body dynamics with RNEA and ABA
  • Differentiable computation with CasADi and JAX
  • Muscle-tendon force mapping for optimization and control

Physics-Constrained OCP/NLP Trajectory Optimization

The optimization framework registers generalized coordinates, velocities, joint torques, muscle activations, contact forces, floating node positions, and reserve torques into a unified variable pool. These variables can be assembled into inverse kinematics, inverse dynamics, trajectory optimization, ground reaction force tracking, and muscle-driven control problems.

  • Direct transcription and integration transcription
  • Modular variable, constraint, and cost blocks
  • Explicit and implicit dynamics formulations

Physics-Constrained Learning-Based Control

Instead of using OCP/NLP outputs only as offline reference trajectories, the optimization module acts as a physics supervisor during policy learning and deployment. The controller learns fast state-to-control mappings, while the optimization framework provides feasibility checks, dynamic residuals, contact consistency constraints, and corrective feedback.

  • Reference trajectories from constrained optimization
  • Physics residuals and contact consistency feedback
  • Improved real-time performance, robustness, and generalization

Preliminary Work

Current Progress

Multibody Trajectory Optimization

Built a preliminary framework that discretizes continuous-time optimal control problems into nonlinear programming problems.

Motion, Torque, and GRF Tracking

Tested motion trajectory input, joint torque optimization, and ground reaction force tracking under coupled contact and physical constraints.

Mixed Muscle-Tendon Branch Design

Proposed an initial muscle model design with active axial muscle elements, passive tendon elements, local Cosserat transition regions, floating nodes, and passive transverse connections.

Evaluation

Tasks, Metrics, and Expected Contributions

Target Tasks

Constrained motion tracking, walking, squatting, and balance recovery.

Metrics

Motion tracking error, GRF error, dynamics residual, contact violation, muscle activation plausibility, control energy, perturbation recovery, and cross-task generalization.

Baselines

Optimization-only methods, learning-only policies, policies without physics supervision, and traditional Hill-type MTU models.

Contributions

A differentiable full-body musculoskeletal modelling and OCP/NLP framework, a controllable-complexity muscle-tendon model, and a hybrid control approach with improved stability, interpretability, and generalization.
